METHODOLOGICAL PROGRESS REPORT — TECHNOLOGY FORESIGHT PIPELINE
DiLab / R&S Regulatory Frequency Monitoring | Status: Post-Midterm Sprint
================================================================================


BACKGROUND
----------

The project builds a structured technology foresight pipeline to generate
plausible future scenarios for regulatory spectrum monitoring by 2035. The
pipeline translates a curated knowledge base of ~45 regulatory, technical, and
policy documents into a set of internally consistent future scenarios, ranked by
strategic relevance for Rohde & Schwarz.

The core methodology is morphological analysis (Zwicky/Ritchey), implemented as
a Weimer-Jehle Cross-Impact Balance (CIB) model. Driver interactions are scored
by a Multi-Perspective Expert panel of four LLM personas representing distinct
professional perspectives (RF engineer, regulatory analyst, R&D strategist,
academic researcher). Monte Carlo sampling over persona disagreement then finds
all internally consistent configurations — the "fixed points" of the system —
which become scenario seeds.


RECENT METHODOLOGICAL IMPROVEMENTS
------------------------------------

1. EVIDENCE-WEIGHTED DRIVER SELECTION

Problem: Driver selection previously used a simple confidence tier
(high/medium/low), which systematically excluded all trend-sourced drivers
regardless of how much evidence supported them.

Change: Replaced the confidence filter with a composite scoring function:

    score = 0.5 x origin_weight + 0.5 x evidence_depth

where origin_weight reflects validation status (cross-confirmed in both BOM and
literature = 1.0; trend-only = 0.4; BOM-only = 0.3) and evidence_depth is the
normalized count of distinct source chunks supporting the driver.

Why it matters: This ensures that well-evidenced environmental drivers (e.g. a
regulatory policy direction supported by 8 sources) compete on equal footing
with hardware drivers confirmed in product specs. The morphological box now
includes both technology-internal and technology-external dimensions, which is a
prerequisite for generating genuinely divergent scenarios.


2. KB COVERAGE-GAP TREND SCANNER (ARCHITECTURAL REPLACEMENT)

Problem: Trend driver extraction previously relied on five hardcoded search
queries (e.g. "AI machine learning spectrum monitoring", "quantum sensing RF
detection"). These queries were technology-focused and domain-specific — they
would produce meaningless results on any different knowledge base, and
critically, they never surfaced the regulatory and policy content already present
in the knowledge base (RSPG strategic visions, WRC-23 Final Acts, ITU-R
SM.2542 proactive monitoring strategy, ECC enforcement benchmarking, etc.).

Change: Replaced hardcoded queries with a bottom-up coverage-gap algorithm:

    1. Embed all BOM driver descriptions to define the "covered" technology space
    2. Retrieve all trend-pool documents from ChromaDB with stored vector
       embeddings
    3. For each document, compute its maximum cosine similarity to any BOM driver
    4. Documents below the similarity threshold (0.55) are "orphans" — content
       the BOM does not explain
    5. K-Means cluster the orphan embeddings (k=12)
    6. For each cluster, an LLM identifies the underlying driver from the most
       representative documents
    7. Post-filter: discard any extracted driver still too similar to a BOM
       driver (threshold 0.70)

Why it matters: This approach is domain-agnostic — it works on any knowledge
base by discovering what topics the BOM does not cover, rather than assuming
which topics matter. Practically, it immediately surfaced the regulatory and
policy dimensions (international harmonization, spectrum sharing mandates,
sovereign reservation frameworks, 5G compliance regimes) that were present in
the knowledge base but invisible to the previous scanner.

Result: The morphological box now contains hardware/technology dimensions (from
BOM) and regulatory/environmental dimensions (from coverage-gap), enabling
scenarios to vary across genuinely independent axes.


3. CIB MATRIX CALIBRATION

Problem: The Multi-Perspective Expert personas were defaulting to non-inhibiting scores
too easily, producing a sparse interaction matrix that caused the consistency
algorithm to converge on a narrow set of fixed points.

Changes:
- Added a 2035 time horizon anchor to the CIB prompt so all personas evaluate
  interactions in the same temporal frame
- Added a DEFAULT RULE requiring explicit justification for any non-inhibiting
  score between drivers that share customer segment, product line, or R&D budget
- Strengthened the regulatory analyst persona with a hard ceiling on
  pro-technology scores without named standards citations

Result: Panel consensus improved from 31 strong / 145 moderate / 6 divergent
to 64 strong / 113 moderate / 5 divergent — more confident assessments, better
differentiated interaction matrix.


4. SCENARIO POOL EXPANSION VIA NEAR-CONSISTENT NEIGHBORS

Problem: Fixed-point analysis previously found only 5 consistent configurations
from ~268 million possible combinations — insufficient for 20 structurally
distinct scenarios.

Change: After finding all fixed points, the pipeline now generates
near-consistent neighbors by performing single-driver-flip variants of each
fixed point and scoring their consistency. This explores the local neighborhood
of each attractor without additional LLM calls.

Result: 129 unique fixed points + ~4,000 near-consistent neighbors. 20 diverse
scenario seeds selected using typed stratification (disruptive / cautionary /
wildcard) and minimum Hamming distance (>=4 driver differences between any two
seeds).


5. MCDA EVALUATION FIX

Problem: The TOPSIS ranking treated risk severity as a benefit criterion —
meaning higher risk ranked better, inverting the intended ranking logic.

Change: Risk severity correctly flagged as a cost criterion in the weighted
benefit/cost vector. AHP weights (Impact > Probability >= Risk Severity >
Time Horizon > Actionability) unchanged.

Result: TOPSIS closeness scores now range 0.36-0.68 with meaningful
differentiation. Top-ranked scenario reflects high impact + high probability +
low risk, as intended.


CURRENT STATE
-------------

The pipeline runs end-to-end in approximately 35 minutes and produces:
- 14 morphological drivers drawn from a mixed hardware + regulatory driver pool
- 106 unique fixed points (consistent system configurations)
- 20 scenario seeds with typed diversity (disruptive / cautionary / wildcard)
- Full traceability from scenario narrative to driver assumption to source document

The central tension in current scenarios has shifted from "how advanced is the
hardware?" to "will the international regulatory environment harmonize or
fragment?" — which is the genuinely relevant strategic question for a company
operating across multiple national spectrum authorities.

Open items for next sprint: reduce redundancy among regulatory clusters
(currently over-fragmented into near-identical sub-topics), prevent the merge
step from combining hardware and regulatory drivers into single morphological
dimensions, and improve wildcard scenario coverage (currently only 2/20).
